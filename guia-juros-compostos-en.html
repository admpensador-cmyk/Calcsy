Complete guide to compound interest

Compound interest is one of the most important concepts in finance. It is often called “interest on interest” because, unlike simple interest, the return from each period is reinvested and begins to earn interest itself. In practice, this means that your wealth grows exponentially over time. Understanding how compound interest works is essential for anyone who wants to invest efficiently.

What is compound interest?

Compound interest is a form of capitalization in which the amount generated at the end of each period is added to the principal and, in the next period, both amounts earn interest together. The general formula is \(M = P \times (1 + i)^n\), where \(M\) is the final amount, \(P\) is the principal (initial value), \(i\) is the rate per period (expressed as a decimal) and \(n\) is the number of periods. When there are periodic contributions, each contribution also grows over time because it is invested at the start of each period and will earn until the end.

The power of compound interest is so important that Albert Einstein reportedly called it the “eighth wonder of the world”. Over the years, small amounts can turn into large sums. The longer the timeframe and the higher the rate, the more pronounced the effect.

Practical example

Imagine investing $1,000 with a monthly contribution of $200 for 12 months at a rate of 1 % per month. At the end of the period, the total will be higher than the sum of the amounts you invested because each time the interest is credited it joins the principal and earns interest again. At first, the difference between simple and compound interest seems small, but over time it becomes significant.

This example shows that, even with relatively small contributions, compound interest helps you reach medium- and long‑term goals. The earlier you start investing, the larger the portion of your final amount will come from the interest. The regularity of your contributions also plays a fundamental role: when you skip a month you not only miss that contribution but also the interest it would have earned.

Tips and cautions

When simulating investments, take inflation and taxes into account. The result presented by the calculator is gross, that is, it does not deduct management fees or income tax where applicable. For a more complete view, compare investment products based on their net rate or find out about taxation (such as the regressive income tax table and the IOF tax on short‑term investments).

Another pitfall is confusing nominal and effective rates. For example, an annual return of 12 % is not exactly the same as 1 % per month when compounding monthly. When converting rates, use specific tools (Calcsy provides an equivalent rate converter). Setting correct parameters prevents projections from straying too far from reality.

Common mistakes and frequently asked questions

Beginners often make a few mistakes when dealing with compound interest. One is to use very optimistic rates based on past returns that are not guaranteed. Another is forgetting that contributions must fit within your budget; there is no point in planning large monthly deposits if you cannot maintain them in practice. Finally, comparing investments without considering risk can lead to bad decisions—equities may have higher long‑term returns but are also subject to fluctuations.

Here are some frequently asked questions:

  • Is compound interest always worthwhile? It accelerates capital growth, but you need to evaluate whether the financial product you choose has a risk appropriate to your profile.
  • Should I reinvest the returns? To take advantage of the compounding effect, yes. Withdrawing the interest reduces the capitalization and slows exponential growth.
  • How do I choose the interest rate? Research the return on similar investments in the market and use conservative values when planning.
